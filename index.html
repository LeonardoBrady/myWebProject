Two cladograms derived from different scientific interpretations of evolutionary relationships of mammals based on morphological and and genetic characters of species. Trends in Ecology and Evolution. 19: 430-438. The left hand cladogram is based on evidence from morphology, while the right hand one is based on evidence from genetics. The two trees are similar, but they are not identical. See if you can identify how many ways these two cladograms differ. This is one of the main difficulties of phylogenetic systematics. The “true” evolutionary tree is lost in the distant past and all that scientists can do is find evidence for the best evolutionary hypothesis. Sometimes different data sets do not give the exact same results. Although we will never know for certain what the true evolutionary tree of any particular clade is the correct one, probability suggests that as more and more evidence is collected the resulting cladograms are closer and closer to the truth.

The Human Lineage

2 / 3

The evolution of humans from Australopithecus to Homo sapiens unfolded over millions of years and is characterized by distinct stages:


  • Australopithecus (4 to 2 million years ago):
    Species like Australopithecus afarensis, exemplified by the fossil "Lucy," demonstrated an upright walking posture but retained some ape-like features.

  • Homo habilis (2.4 to 1.4 million years ago):
    Homo habilis, an early member of the Homo genus, is associated with the use of basic stone tools, representing an early technological leap.

  • Homo erectus (1.9 million to 143,000 years ago):
    Homo erectus exhibited a larger brain size and more advanced stone tools, with evidence suggesting migration out of Africa into Asia and Europe.

  • Archaic Homo sapiens (500,000 to 200,000 years ago):
    Various hominin species in this category displayed a mix of Homo erectus and modern Homo sapiens characteristics, including advancements in tool use and social structures.

  • Homo sapiens (approximately 300,000 years ago to present):
    Homo sapiens, originating in Africa, developed sophisticated tools, art, language, and complex social structures. Migration out of Africa occurred, leading to the global dispersion of Homo sapiens.

Extra info

Key drivers of human evolution included environmental changes, the development of tools and technology, and social factors such as cooperation and communication. The process involved gradual changes in populations over extended periods, shaped by natural selection favoring traits conducive to survival and reproduction. Ongoing research continues to refine our understanding of the specific details and timeline of human evolution.



5 facts why humans have thumbs!

3 / 3
  • Tool Use and Manipulation:
    One of the primary reasons humans evolved to have opposable thumbs is the advantage it provides for manipulating objects and tools. The opposable thumb allows for a precision grip, enabling humans to grasp and use tools with greater dexterity.

  • Fine Motor Skills:
    The development of opposable thumbs contributed to the evolution of fine motor skills. This enhanced manual dexterity allowed early humans to perform intricate tasks, such as crafting tools, creating art, and engaging in complex activities that were crucial for survival and advancement.

  • Tool-Making and Technology:
    Opposable thumbs played a pivotal role in the development of tool-making and technology. The ability to create and use tools is a defining feature of human evolution, influencing our capacity to hunt, build shelters, and adapt to various environments.

  • Adaptation to Environment:
    The opposable thumb provided a significant adaptive advantage in various environments. Whether in forests, grasslands, or other landscapes, the versatility of the human hand, facilitated by the opposable thumb, allowed for effective interaction with diverse surroundings.

  • Social Interaction and Communication:
    The evolution of opposable thumbs is linked to the social nature of humans. The ability to grasp objects, including each other's hands, likely played a role in social bonding and communication. This physical capability might have contributed to the development of complex social structures and cooperation among early human groups.


Extra info

The evolution of opposable thumbs in humans represents a crucial adaptation that has had profound implications for our ability to interact with the environment, create and use tools, and engage in complex social behaviors.
